Hate Speech Analysis

Methodology

The Hate Crimes Team aims to study the topic: "Does negative congressional sentiment influence the number of racial-based hate crimes?" This research is critical in understanding the societal impacts of political rhetoric and how it may have contributed to the rise or fall in hate crimes during 2021 - 2023. We feel it's imperative to unravel any links between the language used in politics and how it shapes societal responses. We can better understand these dynamics and promote more mindful and responsible communication in our political discourse. Our chosen timeframe and carefully selected keywords aim to provide a holistic picture that embraces a wide range of societal challenges. The insights gained from this research could aid in the fight against hate crimes and systemic racism.​​

With the data collected from the C-SPAN Archive and the Federal Bureau of Investigation Crime Data Explorer, we were able to analyze the significance of negative sentiment and the number of racial-based hate crimes in 2021 and 2022. It is noted that data for racial-based hate crimes in 2023 has yet to be published.

African American Hate Crimes

  • Focusing on African American negative sentiment, we see a decline in sentiment with around 48% significance in 2021 but only around 26% in 2022.
  • In contrast, there is a slight increase in hate crimes with around 49% of racial-based hate crimes in 2021 being against African Americans, but in 2022, around 51% of racial-based hate crimes were against African Americans.

Asian American Hate Crimes

  • Focusing on Asian American negative sentiment, we see a heavy decline in sentiment with around 56% of significance residing in 2021 and only around 17% of significance residing in 2022.​​
  • When comparing the significance of crimes against Asian Americans with the number of racial-based hate crimes, we only see a slight decline between the years, with 11.32% in 2021 and 11.23% in 2022.​​
