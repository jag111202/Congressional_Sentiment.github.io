Congressional Polarization

"Polarization: a state in which the opinions, beliefs, or interests of a group or society no longer range along a continuum but become concentrated at opposing extremes" -Merriam-Webster

Acknowledgment

Importance

Increasingly Americans are concerned about polarization on Capitol Hill. With Democrats and Republicans seemingly always at odds, many are worried about whether our democracy can still function. Nowhere is this truer than on the deliberations aired on C-SPAN, where heated exchanges have become the rule, rather than the exception. This project examines whether such exchanges have become more pronounced over the years.

Objectives:

  • Measure sentiment over time using text and audio analysis methods
  • Test whether this is applicable to important issues like hate speech
  • Create interactive graphics of results for public consumption

Central Questions:

  • Has congressional rhetoric become more negative in the Biden vs. Trump administrations?
  • Is this relationship generalizable to discussions of immigration, inflation, and hate speech?

Three Focusses

Sentiment Analysis

"Vader" and "TextBlob" may sound like movie titles, but the way they help analyze sentiment is very real. Comparing these methods to traditional dictionary-based approaches gives the sentiment analysis sub-team a clearer picture of polarization on Capitol Hill.​​

Audio Analysis

When vocal pitch rises, it is often indicative of an activated emotional state caused by anger or fear. To determine whether the tone of speeches has changed over time, the audio analysis sub-team uses speech-to-text software alongside Python modules designed to extract audio features, like vocal pitch.

Hate Speech Analysis

The hate speech sub-team will combine a custom dictionary with other text analysis methods to explore whether discussions of hate crimes have become more polarized.​​
